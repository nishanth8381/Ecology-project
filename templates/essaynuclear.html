Is nuclear energy being exploited to its potential?






NUCLEAR ENERGY!

Nuclear energy is the energy that is released by manipulating atoms.currently there are two ways to produce nuclear energy,through fission(the splitting of its atoms) and fusion(fusing them together).

ONLY NUCLEAR ENERGY CAN SAVE THE PLANET!!...

Nuclear power plant operates basically the same way as a fossil fuel plant, with a major difference THE SOURCE OF HEAT

The process that produces the heat in a nuclear plant is the fission or splitting of uranium atoms. The heat boils water to make steam that turns the turbine-generator, just as in a fossil fuel plant to produce electricity. 

Power plants need to burn the fuel, thereby emissions of green house gases making our environment polluted and increasing the surface temperature, instead , nuclear plants use the heat given off during fission as fuel

Fission takes place at the centre of the reactor core containing uranium fuel(as ceramic pellets)

The ceramic pellets are about size of your fingertip,but each one produces same amount of energy as 150 gallons of oil.

Nuclear reactors are basically machines that contain and control chain reactions, while releasing heat at a controlled rate.

There are lot more advantages of using nuclear energy, like LOW OPERATING COST(relatively cheap to operate)

CLEAN ENERGY(No emissions),HIGH QUANTITY,RELIABILITY,CONCENTRATION(most concentrated form of energy produces large quantity in short time).

Unlike other power plants nuclear power plant is not dependent on local sources,can be set up any part of the globe if the uranium were mined and transported in a more efficient way.

Nuclear power is dirty, dangerous and expensive; not carbon-free; and encourages nuclear proliferation. A nuclear power station itself does not emit greenhouse gasses like CO2. Yet nuclear power contributes to climate change; with every step in the whole fuel chain, needed to generate electricity in the end, many energy is used. For instance, the extraction of uranium and the enrichment of uranium are extremely energy-intensive processes. Life-cycle analysis of the whole fuel chain clearly shows the contribution of nuclear power to climate change. In a ground-breaking study Sovacool et al screened 103 lifecycle studies of greenhouse gas equivalent emissions for nuclear power plants to identify a subset of the most current, original, and transparent studies. They calculated that while the range of emissions for nuclear energy over the lifetime of a plant reported from qualified studies examined is from 1.4 grams of carbon dioxide equivalent per kWh (gCO2e/kWh) to 288 gCO2e/kWh, the mean value is 66 gCO2e/kWh.
